02 — What I believe
№1

Fast is a feature.

Every second of load time costs somebody a sale. I treat Core Web Vitals as a design constraint, not a post-launch chore — image budgets, lean plugin stacks, and caching decided before the first section is built.

№2

Converting beats impressing.

A website is an employee. It should answer the visitor's question, remove their doubt, and ask for the inquiry. Beautiful is table stakes; the layouts I build are sequenced around one job — the next click.

№3

Hand the keys back.

A build that only its developer can maintain is a liability with good typography. I structure every project so the owner can update content, products, and pages without me — then they hire me back for the work that matters.

Case 01

The Arch International

thearchinternational.com
Role

Sole designer & developer

Year

Full platform build

thearchinternational.com
THE ARCH INTERNATIONALassets/arch.jpg

Challenge

An international organization needed its entire digital presence built from zero — no existing site to lean on, no design system, no content structure. Everything from information architecture to launch was on one pair of hands.

Thinking

Organizations like this live or die on clarity: a first-time visitor has to understand the mission, find the program they care about, and know how to act within one scroll. I architected the sitemap around those three motions before opening a page builder.

Build

Custom WordPress build with a consistent template system in Elementor Pro — global styles, reusable section patterns, mobile-first breakpoints. Performance was treated as a launch requirement, not an optimization phase.

Outcome

Shipped in 2025 and running since. The organization's team updates programs and pages themselves; I stay on for structural work — the handoff pattern working exactly as designed.

Lesson kept: the sitemap is the product. Everything after it is execution.

Rose Bookshop

Role

Developer, ongoing

Year

2024 → present

Stack

WooCommerce · Elementor · custom plugin

E-commerce

rosebookshop.com
ROSE BOOKSHOPassets/rose.jpg

Challenge

An independent bookshop needed a real store, not a brochure — full catalog, secure checkout, payment gateways, and browsing that feels closer to shelf-wandering than database-querying.

Thinking

Book buyers browse in bursts. Forcing a full page load for every title kills the wandering instinct that sells books, so the catalog needed a way to peek at a book without leaving the shelf.

Build

WooCommerce store built with Elementor Pro, plus a custom product popup plugin written for exactly that browsing problem — tap a cover, preview the book in place, keep scrolling. Secure checkout and gateway configuration behind it.

Outcome

Selling since 2024, with an ongoing maintenance relationship covering product updates and storefront changes. When they need something, they don't post a job — they message me.

Lesson kept: when the off-the-shelf widget doesn't exist, a small custom plugin beats a big compromise.

Case 03

JB Dondolo

jbdondolo.org
Role

Rescue developer, ongoing

Year

Ongoing

Stack

WordPress · Avada · Divi

Type

Nonprofit rescue & care

jbdondolo.org
JB DONDOLOassets/jbdondolo.jpg

Challenge

A live nonprofit site running Avada and Divi builders side by side — a setup that guarantees conflicts — with plugin clashes breaking layouts while real donors were trying to give.

Thinking

On a rescue, the instinct to rebuild everything is usually wrong. A nonprofit can't afford downtime or a redesign bill; it needs the bleeding stopped in the right order, with the donation flow protected above all else.

Build

Systematic conflict isolation — reproduce, bisect plugins, identify the cross-builder collisions — then a stabilization pass: pruning the stack, resolving the conflicts, and hardening what remained.

Outcome

The site is stable and the donation flow reliable. The engagement never ended — I remain their developer for performance, updates, and new sections.

Lesson kept: rescues are triage, not surgery. Fix in the order the business bleeds.

06 — How an engagement runs

Understand the business first

Before any build talk: what does this site have to do for the money? Who lands on it, and what should they do next? Scope gets challenged here if the brief and the goal disagree.

Architect before building

Sitemap, content structure, and the performance budget — decided up front. The expensive mistakes in web projects are all architectural, and they're all cheapest on day one.

Build in daily light

Short end-of-day updates: what got done, what's next, what I need. Clients never wonder what's happening — it's the habit my reviews mention most.

Ship fast, verify faster

Launch includes the checks most builds skip: Core Web Vitals, structured data validation, analytics events firing, forms delivering, every page clicked through on a real phone.

Hand the keys back

Documentation and a walkthrough so the owner can run their own site. Then I stay available — most engagements on this page never actually ended.
